# Infected Origins — Role of the Mother

## Core problem

The mother must not be only “the sick wife” who motivates the plot. If she is only a passive illness-object, the father's fall becomes thinner and the daughter’s transformation loses moral weight.

## Best function for the mother

She is the emotional and moral center of the family. She wants to live, but not at any cost.

Her core belief:

> “A cure that costs someone else their life is not a cure. It is only a trade.”

This directly opposes the father’s later logic.

---

## Mother’s arc

### 1. Warmth and clarity

At the beginning, she is sick but mentally present. She jokes, comforts, observes. She sees that the father and daughter are both brilliant, but also both vulnerable to obsession.

She should not be written as helpless. Physically weak, yes. Morally weak, no.

### 2. She plants the Lilith symbol

The Lilith mythology image can come from her.

Possible scene:

The daughter asks about the image. The mother says Lilith was turned into a monster by people who wanted her obedient — but then adds that becoming free and becoming cruel are not the same thing.

Possible line:

> “Sie wurde zum Monster gemacht, weil sie nicht gehorchen wollte. Aber pass auf: Manchmal benutzt man diese Geschichte, um sich alles zu erlauben.”

This line becomes tragic when the daughter later adopts the name.

### 3. She recognizes the father’s change first

The mother notices before the daughter does that the father’s language is changing.

Early father language:

- healing
- patient
- your mother
- chance

Later father language:

- compatibility
- subject
- protocol
- necessary loss

Possible line:

> “Du redest nicht mehr davon, mich zu retten. Du redest davon, recht zu behalten.”

### 4. She sets the moral boundary

She directly tells him and/or the daughter that she does not want to be saved through harm to others.

Possible line:

> “Ich will leben. Aber nicht, wenn ihr dafür jemand anderes begraben müsst.”

This makes the father's later abduction order more tragic, because he is no longer acting for her wishes. He is acting against them while using her as justification.

### 5. She becomes the daughter's last anchor

Before the daughter discovers the truth, the mother gives her a warning:

> “Versprich mir, dass du ihn nicht rettest, indem du wirst wie er.”

The daughter may promise. The ending then breaks that promise.

### 6. What happens to her?

Three viable options:

#### Option A — She dies before the final act

Her death pushes both father and daughter over the edge.

Risk: too melodramatic if timed badly.

Best use: she dies because the father delayed honest care while chasing the GenetiX cure, making his guilt worse.

#### Option B — She dies because of the treatment

The father’s experimental cure fails or harms her. This is brutal and thematically strong: the thing he justified everything for destroys the person he wanted to save.

Best line after:

> “Sie war nicht das erste Opfer deines Projekts. Sie war nur das erste, das du geliebt hast.”

#### Option C — She survives but rejects the daughter

Darkest psychological version. After the daughter becomes Lilith, the mother is alive enough to see what her daughter has become and rejects her.

Possible ending echo:

Mother:

> “Das bist nicht du.”

Daughter/Lilith:

> “Doch. Endlich.”

Risk: may complicate continuity unless the mother is hidden, dies later, or becomes a secret wound.

---

## Decision

The mother **dies**. This gives both the father and the daughter another impulse of grief, panic, and desperation.

Best version for this story: **Option B** — she dies because the father’s experimental treatment/GenetiX protocol fails or accelerates the illness. This creates maximum tragedy:

- The father’s entire justification collapses.
- He cannot admit it, so he doubles down.
- The daughter sees that the research already killed the person it was meant to save.
- Her later decision to continue the research becomes darker: she is not saving her mother anymore; she is preserving meaning from the loss.
- The mother’s death becomes a shared wound, but father and daughter process it differently: he becomes more frantic and guilty; she becomes more suspicious, grief-hardened, and eventually more controlling.

Her death should happen before the father orders the abduction or shortly after the first major human experiment. Strongest placement: shortly before the abduction order, so his next moral collapse is powered by grief and the need to prove the death was not meaningless.

The father’s self-lie after her death:

> “If I stop now, she died for nothing.”

The daughter later inherits the same self-lie:

> “If I stop now, he died for nothing.”

That mirror is the point.
