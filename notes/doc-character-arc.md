# Infected Origins — Doc Character Arc

## Core idea from Logge (2026-06-07)

The prequel centers on **a doctor**. He starts as a fundamentally good person: a caring family father, not a cartoon villain. Over the film, he should be pushed through psychological pressure until his later evil/antagonistic behavior becomes understandable, even if not excusable.

## Working dramatic thesis

> A good person does not become evil because he suddenly stops loving people. He becomes dangerous when fear, responsibility, guilt, and institutional pressure convince him that cruelty is the only way to protect what still matters to him.

## Recommended arc: from healer to rationalizer

1. **Warm introduction**
   - He is shown with his family: attentive father, maybe tired but loving.
   - His original medical motivation is legitimate: curing something, saving children, preventing an outbreak, protecting his own family.

2. **The first compromise**
   - He joins or stays with the organization because the research has real potential.
   - He witnesses something ethically wrong but frames it as temporary: “If I leave now, worse people take over.”

3. **Isolation and pressure**
   - The organization controls information, career, money, or his family’s safety.
   - His daughter initially helps him: emotionally, practically, or even scientifically/administratively. She believes in him because she sees the loving father and desperate husband, not the monster he may become.
   - As he does more work for GenetiX, he increasingly shuts her out. At first he hides details to protect her; later he hides them because he knows she would judge him.
   - He is slowly separated from normal moral feedback: fewer family scenes, more sterile lab scenes.

4. **Personal trigger: sick wife**
   - His wife is seriously ill, and he is researching a cure for her.
   - This keeps his motivation human and painful: he is not chasing power at first, he is trying to save the person he loves.
   - The scientific community's rejection therefore hurts twice: they are not only humiliating his work, they are, in his mind, condemning his wife.
   - This is where the audience understands him emotionally.

4a. **GenetiX attacks**
   - The antagonistic company **GenetiX** uses his humiliation as a recruitment/opening.
   - They do not approach him as cartoon villains; they validate him: “They laughed because they are afraid you might be right.”
   - GenetiX offers funding, labs, protection, and the chance to continue without ethics boards slowing him down.
   - Their first manipulation is emotional: they make him feel seen, brilliant, and betrayed by the scientific establishment.
   - Their second manipulation is practical: they give him exactly what he needs when everyone else has abandoned him.

5. **The irreversible act**
   - He authorizes or performs one act he previously would have condemned.
   - It should be framed as “one life for many” or “one terrible thing to prevent a worse thing.”

6. **Moral self-deception**
   - He stops saying “this is wrong” and starts saying “this is necessary.”
   - He no longer sees victims as people first; they become cases, samples, liabilities.

7. **End state for Infected continuity**
   - By the time Klara/Frank are involved, he has crossed the line.
   - He may still love his family, but that love has become the justification for everything monstrous.

## Psychological tools that fit the tone

- **Guilt:** He believes earlier hesitation caused someone’s death.
- **Fear:** The serum/project may be the only perceived way to protect his family.
- **Humiliation by the scientific community:** His work is mocked publicly, his papers are rejected, colleagues treat him as a dangerous crank or failed genius. This does not make him evil by itself, but it becomes emotional fuel: he wants to prove them wrong.
- **Wounded pride / recognition hunger:** He starts wanting to save people, but slowly also wants the world to admit he was right.
- **Cognitive dissonance:** Every compromise must be made to feel like the last one.
- **Authority pressure:** Superiors/lab leads force decisions, then make him complicit.
- **Sunk cost:** “If we stop now, everyone who died died for nothing.”
- **Dehumanization through paperwork:** names become numbers, patients become datasets.
- **Sleep deprivation / stress:** not as a cheap insanity trope, but as a way to erode judgment.

## Important warning

Do **not** make him “crazy” out of nowhere. The stronger version is: he remains emotionally understandable and even loving, while his moral logic becomes corrupted.

## Possible connection to existing characters

- Could become or be connected to **Dr. Susan** / **Dr. Lilith** as the project’s medical authority.
- If he is a new doctor character, he can be the origin point of the serum and the person whose decisions create the conditions for Susan/Lilith’s later coldness.
- His family can mirror the innocent family in the original film, making the later forest-family collateral damage more thematically painful.

## Daughter succession twist

Logge's new direction: the doctor's daughter becomes the true continuation of the project.

### Setup

- The daughter helps him at first because she believes he is trying to save her mother.
- She is intelligent enough to understand parts of the research and emotionally loyal enough to ignore early warning signs.
- As he works more for GenetiX, he shuts her out. This turns her from helper into investigator.

### Laboratory confrontation

- Near the end, she confronts him in the lab.
- She discovers the actual state of his research: the cure has mutated into cruel, unethical, possibly lethal virus/serum experiments.
- They argue. The emotional center is not just “you are evil,” but: “You used Mom as an excuse.”
- In the struggle/rangelei, he dies by accident. This should feel tragic, not like she intentionally murders him.

### GenetiX exploitation and daughter's transition

- GenetiX immediately sees the daughter as useful: she is brilliant, already familiar with parts of the work, and psychologically vulnerable.
- Important: she has already researched a lot with her father. She is not randomly chosen; she genuinely understands the science, the notes, and parts of the method.
- GenetiX approaches her after the father's death and she initially appears enthusiastic. On the surface, she plays along: grateful, impressed, eager to continue his work.
- This enthusiasm is strategic and emotionally complicated. She is not simply pretending; part of her really does want access to the lab and wants to continue her father's research.
- She acts hard, ambitious, and increasingly obsessed so GenetiX believes she is on their side. She lets them think they successfully recruited her.
- Internally, she blames GenetiX for corrupting and destroying her father. Her performance is revenge disguised as succession.
- Her key contradiction: she hates GenetiX, but she also refuses to abandon the research because it is the last living piece of her father and possibly still the cure for her mother.
- This solves the transition: she does not suddenly become evil. She learns to weaponize GenetiX's own cold logic against them, then keeps the machine because she still believes the work must continue.
- Their mistake: they assume she is broken in the same way he was and therefore controllable. In reality, she is colder, smarter, and more intentional.

### Revenge / takeover ending

- The daughter realizes GenetiX is responsible for corrupting her father and enabling the research.
- During the presentation of the new serum/virus/cure candidate, she gathers the entire board/executive leadership in one room.
- She poisons/infects them all with the virus.
- Afterward, she takes over the lab/company and continues the research.
- Continuity direction: she becomes the doctor figure from the main film who organizes the hunt for the infected fugitive. This likely makes her the prequel origin/version of **Dr. Lilith** or the person behind Lilith's later authority.

### Why this works

- The film becomes a generational corruption story: the father is destroyed by humiliation, grief, and GenetiX; the daughter is destroyed by grief, betrayal, and revenge.
- She starts as the moral witness and ends as the new monster.
- The ending is darker because GenetiX does not die with the board; its logic survives through her.
