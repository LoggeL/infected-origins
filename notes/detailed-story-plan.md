# Infected Origins — Detailed Story Plan

## Current core premise

A respected but increasingly desperate doctor researches a cure for his severely ill wife. His daughter helps him because she believes in him and in the hope of saving her mother. After the scientific community humiliates him and cuts him off, the antagonistic biotech company **GenetiX** exploits his wounded pride, fear, and love for his family. His cure research mutates into unethical serum/virus experiments.

The daughter eventually discovers what he has become. In a lab confrontation, he dies accidentally during a struggle. GenetiX tries to recruit her as his replacement. She plays along, acts ambitious and loyal, gathers the board for a presentation, infects/poisons them with the virus, then takes over the company/lab to continue her father's research. She becomes the doctor figure who later organizes the hunt for the infected fugitive in the main film.

- The mother should play an active role, not just be the sick motivation.

## Main critique points to solve

### 1. Daughter's transformation must be gradual

The danger: she goes from moral daughter to murderous successor too quickly.

Solution: her transformation needs three layers:

1. **Love:** She wants to save her mother and preserve her father's work.
2. **Betrayal:** She realizes GenetiX corrupted her father and used his desperation.
3. **Arrogance:** She believes she can control the research better than he and GenetiX did.

Her self-lie:

> “My father lost himself because they poisoned the work. I will keep the work pure.”

This is tragic because she repeats the same pattern while thinking she is correcting it.

### 2. GenetiX must need her, not just randomly choose her

The danger: why would a powerful company hand responsibility to the grieving daughter?

Solution: establish early that she is essential:

- She helped decode or stabilize part of the formula.
- Her father used her idea but never fully credited her.
- She understands his notebooks better than anyone.
- GenetiX realizes the research cannot continue without her.
- She can reproduce a missing step or interpret an encrypted lab notebook.

This makes her recruitment practical, not sentimental.

### 3. The board poisoning needs a believable setup

The danger: if the board all gathers and gets infected, they look stupid.

Solution: make the meeting a high-stakes staged demonstration:

- GenetiX executives believe she will unveil the stabilized cure/serum.
- They are greedy and want proof before approving full deployment.
- She frames it as a private, controlled demo because the breakthrough is too sensitive.
- The infection delivery can be disguised as a safety protocol, inhalation test, sealed-room aerosol, or “neutralized sample” exposure.
- They trust her because she has convincingly performed loyalty and obsession.

### 4. Her motive to continue research after revenge must be crystal clear

The danger: if she sees the research as evil, why continue it?

Solution: she separates the people from the project.

Her logic:

> “GenetiX was evil. The research is not. My father’s dream is still worth saving.”

But the audience should see the irony: this is exactly the same rationalization her father used.

### 5. The main-film connection needs concrete continuity hooks

By the end of the prequel, the following should be visibly in place:

- GenetiX or its successor structure still exists under her control.
- The green serum/virus exists.
- Black-vein infection symptoms are established.
- Frank is introduced or selected as a future subject.
- The doctrine exists: escaped infected subjects must be hunted and contained.
- She has authority over guards/security.
- Her coldness in the main film has a personal origin.

## Proposed act structure

## Act I — The good doctor, the sick wife, and the daughter before Lilith

### Opening image

A normal home. The doctor is loving but exhausted. His wife is sick. His daughter helps around the house and with small research tasks. The family feels real, not just plot motivation.

Important symbolic prop: somewhere in the house, office, or later GenetiX space hangs an image/painting connected to **Lilith mythology**. At first it is just background: a woman associated with independence, exile, night, rebellion, or demonization. Later, the daughter consciously takes this name/identity on.

Possible scenes:

- Daughter brings father coffee while he works late.
- Wife jokes weakly that they both need sleep.
- The wife is emotionally active: she notices the father's obsession before everyone else and gently warns the daughter not to lose herself in his mission.
- The mother may be the one who explains or comments on the Lilith image: not as a villain symbol, but as a story about a woman who refuses to be controlled or defined by others.
- The doctor records a private video log: “Trial compound 17 shows response, but degradation is too fast.”
- Daughter notices a mistake in his calculation or labels a sample correctly. This plants that she is smart and involved.

### Scientific humiliation

He presents his work to colleagues or a review board. They dismiss him.

Possible beats:

- Someone calls it “desperation dressed as medicine.”
- A former mentor says: “You are too close to this emotionally.”
- Funding is denied.
- A paper is rejected because the method is risky/unethical.

Important: the critics should not be completely wrong. They should have valid ethical concerns. That makes the story less cartoonish.

### GenetiX approaches

GenetiX contacts him after the humiliation.

They say the exact thing he needs to hear:

> “They laughed because they are afraid you might be right.”

They offer funding, a lab, access, and speed. He resists at first, then agrees because his wife is running out of time.

Act I turn:

> He signs with GenetiX, telling himself it is only until he saves her.

---

## Act II — The cure becomes the project

### Early GenetiX phase

At first, the lab looks like salvation. Clean, funded, serious. The doctor is productive again. Daughter is cautiously hopeful.

Possible scenes:

- Daughter visits and is impressed by the equipment.
- GenetiX compliments her intelligence.
- Father and daughter solve a problem together.
- The wife improves slightly or a test result suggests hope.

### First ethical compromise

GenetiX pushes him from legal/ethical testing into a grey area.

Examples:

- Animal trials are skipped.
- Human tissue samples are obtained suspiciously.
- A terminal patient “volunteers” under questionable pressure.
- Data is manipulated to keep the project alive.

The doctor objects, but GenetiX frames it as urgency:

> “Every delay is another patient dying.”

### Daughter notices the shift

He becomes secretive.

Visual progression:

- At home: warm light, open doors, shared notes.
- Later: locked drawers, passworded files, cold lab lighting.
- He stops explaining details to her.
- He says: “You wouldn’t understand.”

That line should hurt because she does understand.

### Escalation into virus/serum work

The cure begins behaving like something else: aggressive regeneration, vascular mutation, black-vein symptoms, contamination risk.

The doctor rationalizes:

> “It is unstable because it is powerful.”

GenetiX starts seeing military/commercial potential. They are less interested in the wife and more interested in control, patents, weaponization, containment.

Midpoint:

> The daughter discovers a hidden file or live test proving the work has become human experimentation.

## Act III — The daughter becomes the successor

### The father's worst order: abduction

Before the laboratory confrontation, the father crosses the line that makes his fall undeniable: he orders or authorizes an abduction.

This should be the moment where the audience understands he has become what he once hated.

Possible framing:

- GenetiX presents a target profile as “the only viable match.”
- The father hesitates because the target is young/innocent.
- GenetiX pressures him: without this subject, his wife dies and the work collapses.
- He gives the order indirectly at first: “Do what is necessary.”
- Later it becomes explicit enough that the daughter can discover his responsibility.

This can directly foreshadow or initiate the continuity chain toward Klara's abduction in the main film.

### Laboratory confrontation

The daughter confronts him in the lab.

Core emotional argument:

Daughter:

> “You said this was for Mom.”

Father:

> “It still is.”

Daughter:

> “Then why did you order them to take someone else's child?”

Father:

> “Because she was compatible.”

Daughter:

> “No. Because you needed another excuse.”

Father:

> “You don't understand what it means to be this close.”

Daughter:

> “I understood everything. That is why you locked me out.”

He should not be a full monster. He should be broken, defensive, ashamed, and still convinced stopping would make every sacrifice meaningless.

The struggle:

- She tries to destroy data or take evidence.
- He tries to stop her.
- They fight over a sample, tablet, vial, or control switch.
- Accident: he falls, is exposed, hits his head, or triggers equipment.
- He dies in a way tied to his research.

Last possible line from him:

> “Don’t let them take it.”

This line is dangerous because she interprets it as permission to continue.

### GenetiX recruits her

GenetiX covers up the accident and approaches her.

They say:

> “Your father was brilliant, but unstable. You can finish what he started.”

She performs exactly what they want:

- cold grief
- ambition
- technical competence
- resentment toward the scientific establishment
- willingness to continue

But inside, she is planning revenge.

### The board presentation — expanded

She requests full leadership access for a live demonstration of the stabilized compound.

Set up her performance:

- She acts obsessed with results.
- She speaks like her father, but colder.
- She uses GenetiX language: containment, assets, viable subjects, deployment.
- She lets them believe grief has made her useful, not dangerous.
- She frames the presentation as the moment where GenetiX can finally own the breakthrough that the scientific community rejected.

Why the board gathers:

- They are not random idiots in a room; this is a private emergency investor/board demonstration after the father's death.
- The project is too sensitive for normal reports.
- They need to decide whether to shut it down, militarize it, or scale it.
- She claims only a live closed-room demonstration can prove stability.
- Greed and control bring them there.

Possible infection method:

- A “neutralized” aerosol demonstration in a sealed presentation lab.
- A ceremonial microdose/immune marker test that she claims is safe.
- Contaminated safety masks or injector pens.
- The room's containment system locks under the pretext of protocol.

The reveal:

- At first, the board thinks the demonstration worked.
- Then symptoms appear: black veins, coughing, panic.
- She watches coldly, not because she enjoys it, but because she believes this is judgment.
- This should be morally horrifying, not purely triumphant. The audience should feel: yes, GenetiX deserved punishment, but she has crossed a line.

This is also the moment she fully takes the Lilith identity. The myth image/name returns: she chooses the name not as decoration, but as rebirth after betrayal.

### Final takeover

After the board dies, she does not destroy the lab.

She cleans it up.
She secures the data.
She gives orders.
She becomes the system.

Possible final line:

> “My father wanted permission. I don’t.”

Or:

> “The work continues.”

## Final continuity bridge into Infected

Final montage / epilogue can show:

- Guards being trained under new containment rules.
- The green serum being prepared.
- A file labeled **FRANK** or a patient in an OP gown.
- A white van in the garage.
- A child/family target file that foreshadows Klara.
- The daughter/doctor saying: “If a subject escapes, no witnesses.”

This lands directly in the main film's world.

---

## Strongest thematic version

The story is not “good family becomes evil family.”

It is:

> A father sacrifices morality to save his wife. A daughter condemns him for it, then inherits the same logic in the name of saving his legacy.

That is the tragedy.
